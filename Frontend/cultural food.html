Featured Food Story

The Spice Route: A Journey Through Kerala's Cardamom Hills

By Priya Sharma, Food Historian

Long before the Portuguese navigator Vasco da Gama set foot on the Malabar Coast in 1498, Kerala was already a bustling center of the global spice trade. Cardamom, pepper, cinnamon, cloves, and nutmeg from these fertile hills had made their way to the kitchens of Rome, the markets of Arabia, and the dining tables of distant China.

My journey began in the misty cardamom plantations of Thekkady, where the morning air carries an intoxicating aroma of spices. A local farmer named Thomas, whose family has cultivated cardamom for seven generations, guided me through the emerald-green slopes.

"Each cardamom pod is harvested by hand," Thomas explained as he carefully plucked the green pods. "We pick them just before they're fully ripe to preserve their essential oils. This is why Kerala cardamom is renowned worldwide for its intense fragrance."

In the nearby village of Kumily, I visited traditional spice markets where traders still use ancient measuring techniques and bargaining practices that haven't changed for centuries. Here, the culinary heritage of Kerala comes alive in dishes like appam with stew, karimeen pollichathu (pearl spot fish wrapped in banana leaf), and the fiery fish curry with kudampuli (Malabar tamarind).

The most memorable food experience came in a small home kitchen in Kumarakom, where I learned to prepare a traditional Syrian Christian duck roast. The complex blend of spices – cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, star anise, and stone flower – created layers of flavor that told the story of Kerala's multicultural history through a single dish.

As we feasted on the duck roast with appam and vegetable stew, my host Mariam shared stories of how recipes have been preserved through generations, with subtle variations marking each family's unique culinary signature. "Our food is our history," she said. "Each dish carries the memory of trade routes, cultural exchanges, and family traditions."
